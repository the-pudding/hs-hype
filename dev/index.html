The Hype Ain’t Right

The top high school basketball players get lots of hype about being the next big thing. But how often does that work out?

By Russell Goldenberg & Amber Thomas

🏀

Feb 2019

Remember that NBA great Donnell Harvey? Neither do we. Despite being the number one high school recruit in 2000—something that you think would indicate future NBA prowess—he put together a meager career average around 5 points per game over 5 years. On the flip side is LeBron James—the number one high school player just a few years later—now the *greatest NBA player of all-time.

So this begs the question, do we really know in high school how these players will turn out, or is it all just hype?

Hype is even more inflated in the YouTube era. Sure LeBron had some games televised in high school, but now millions of people are watching Zion throw down dunk after monstrous dunk, or Lamelo Ball hit threes from half court in eight grade.

Let’s take a look at just how far the high school stars of the past 20 years made it in their basketball careers.

Below, each circle represents a single player that ranked amongst the Top 100 high school players in the US.

Looks like only about 28% of the 1,563 top ranked high school players made it to the NBA and only 17% made it past their two-year rookie contract. A slim 3 players—LeBron James, Chris Paul, and James Harden—eventually reached an average vorp score of 2 or higher, claiming “AllStar” status. But perhaps the top 100 is too broad of a categorization. Surely the top 10 players will make it to the NBA more often, right?

Players ranked in the top 10 certainly appear to have slightly better odds—a full 84% of them were eventually drafted to the NBA. In the late 90’s and early 2000’s, teams could bet hard enough on a player’s future success that they could draft them straight out of high school. This ended in 2006 when a rule was put into place, declaring that players had to be at least 19 years old or one year removed from high school before getting drafted. But since there are talks about reversing this rule as early as 2021, let’s look at the success of the straight-to-NBA high school players.

Opponents of removing the age limit often cite players like Korleone Young as reasons to keep it in place. Young, the #3 ranked high school player in 1998, ultimately fizzled out of the NBA having played only 15 minutes across 3 games. However, he appears to be the exception more than the rule. Of the other 34 players drafted straight out of high school, 74% made it past their rookie contract, with 69% eventually becoming great NBA players.

Let’s switch our view once more, away from the highest ranking high school players to those that played in a US high school, but never made it onto the Top 100 list. We found 338 of these players that were eventually drafted into the NBA, despite the tepid high school start. Let’s compare their career paths with our highly-ranked and drafted high schoolers.

Even when we limit the top ranked players to just those that were eventually drafted, the success rate of players ranked in high school is notably higher than for those who weren’t. Where all 441 drafted and ranked players made it past their rookie contract, 70% of the unranked and drafted players left the NBA before they had gotten 2 years of playtime under their belts. Two players, Russell Westbrook and Stephen Curry, whose high school careers were apparently not worthy of a spot amongst the top 100, still broke into the ranks of “AllStars” in their NBA careers.
